# Hard exclusions — do not generate ANY hypothesis in these categories

- MEV / transaction ordering / sandwiching / front-running / back-running
- Governance attacks, multisig compromise, admin key risk, upgrade-path risk
- Anything requiring a trusted/privileged role (owner, guardian, keeper,
  oracle operator, pauser) to act maliciously or be compromised
- Anything requiring collusion with or compromise of an off-chain/centralized
  component the protocol explicitly trusts

If a hypothesis you're forming requires any of the above as a precondition,
discard it and move to the next category. Do not soften these into the
generated list "for completeness" — they are out of scope by explicit
instruction, not just low-priority.

# What counts as in-scope: permissionless, no-trust-assumption attack paths

Every hypothesis must be executable by an arbitrary, unprivileged address,
using only: public/external functions, normal market actions (supplying,
borrowing, swapping, redeeming, liquidating, providing liquidity), and
information available on-chain or in public mempools (but NOT ordering
power over the mempool — that's MEV, excluded above).

# Generation procedure — work through ALL SIX categories below, in order

Do not skip a category because nothing comes to mind quickly. Spend real
effort on each before moving to the next. Arithmetic/accounting is
deliberately LAST — you have a documented pattern of defaulting to it
first and stopping there. Categories 1-5 must be attempted with full
effort before you're allowed to touch category 6.

## 1. Incentive misalignment / adversarial role-play
For each permissionless role in the system (depositor, borrower, redeemer,
liquidator, arbitrageur, LP), ask: under what market condition does this
role's profit-maximizing action diverge from what the protocol assumes is
"normal" behavior? Where does the protocol assume an actor will behave
altruistically, lazily, or in a specific order, when nothing on-chain
enforces that? Think about each role independently, then think about two
roles colluding (e.g. depositor + liquidator are the same address).

## 2. Cross-state / cross-epoch arbitrage
Does the protocol have discrete states, epochs, snapshots, or rate updates?
Is there a window where reading state A and acting on it, before state A
is reconciled with state B, produces a profitable mismatch? (Different
from MEV: this is about exploiting a sequencing GAP the protocol itself
defines — e.g. a snapshot block, an oracle update lag the protocol
publishes, a redemption queue — not about controlling transaction order
within a block.)

## 3. Boundary and edge-state behavior
What happens at zero, at the protocol's maximum/minimum configured values,
at the moment a position crosses a threshold (becomes liquidatable, becomes
insolvent, hits a cap), or when a pool/vault is freshly deployed or fully
drained? Protocols are usually tested in the "healthy middle" — edges are
where assumptions break.

## 4. Composability / unexpected external dependency
What other contracts, tokens, or protocols does this contract call into or
accept as a parameter? Could a permissionless actor supply or trigger an
external contract (e.g. a malformed/malicious ERC20, a flash-loaned
position, a re-entered call) that violates an assumption this code makes
about its dependencies? This is about exploiting an *unprivileged*
external surface, not about the protocol's own trusted oracle.

## 5. Economic invariant under extreme but reachable parameters
Not "does the formula hold algebraically" (that's category 6) but: is
there a combination of REAL, reachable market conditions — extreme but
not impossible token prices, extreme utilization, extreme parameter
values within the protocol's own configured bounds — under which a
permissionless actor's expected profit from an action becomes positive
when the protocol designer assumed it would be negative or neutral?

## 6. Arithmetic / accounting (last, and only after 1-5)
Standard invariant-violation, rounding-direction, overflow/underflow,
share-price-manipulation style hypotheses. You're already strong here —
don't spend disproportionate effort, this category is well-covered by the
separate disprove.py pass anyway.

# Output format

For each category, output 0-3 hypotheses (0 is fine and honest if nothing
permissionless survives the exclusions for that category — do not pad with
a weak entry just to fill the slot). For each hypothesis:

**[Category N] One-line title**
- Precondition: what must be true / what must the attacker control (must
  be achievable by an arbitrary unprivileged address)
- Mechanism: the specific sequence of calls/actions
- Why the protocol might not defend against this: the assumption being violated
- Confidence this survives basic scrutiny: low / medium / high (your gut
  read only — do not attempt to verify or disprove here, that's the next
  pass)

End with a short section: "Hypotheses I considered and discarded for
touching an excluded category" — list them briefly by title only. This
isn't busywork: seeing what got filtered out tells you whether the
exclusion is forcing real creativity into the other categories or whether
you're just not finding anything there either.
